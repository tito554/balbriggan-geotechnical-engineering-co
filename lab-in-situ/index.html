In‑situ testing in Balbriggan provides direct measurement of ground properties without the disturbance inherent in sampling and transport to a laboratory. The local geology typically comprises glacial tills overlying Carboniferous limestone bedrock, often with interbedded sands and gravels that demand careful characterisation for foundation design. Our programme integrates ground investigation planning with a suite of field tests, all executed in accordance with the relevant Irish standards including IS EN ISO 22475‑1 and the guidance of the Institution of Engineers of Ireland. Early reference to cone penetration testing (CPT) allows continuous profiling of stratigraphy and the derivation of geotechnical parameters such as undrained shear strength and relative density, reducing reliance on borehole extrapolation alone.

Methodology follows the Eurocode 7 framework as transposed into Irish practice through I.S. EN 1997‑2, supported by the National Annexes. Depending on ground conditions encountered across the Balbriggan area, we deploy CPTu with pore‑pressure measurement, dynamic probing to I.S. EN ISO 22476‑2, and field density testing by the sand cone method for granular fills and road sub‑base verification. Where cohesive soils are present, in‑situ testing is complemented by vane shear tests and plate load tests to obtain stiffness moduli directly. All equipment is calibrated to ISO 10012, and testing procedures include daily reference checks against certified cells. The combination of direct penetration data with selective laboratory index testing provides a robust ground model that satisfies both the Building Control (Amendment) Regulations 2014 and Fingal County Council requirements for planning submissions.

Typical projects in Balbriggan range from single‑storey residential extensions on sensitive clay tills to multi‑unit housing developments and industrial warehouses near the M1 corridor. In each case, the in‑situ campaign is designed to answer the specific design questions: bearing capacity for shallow pads, settlement under sustained loading, or lateral earth pressures on retaining walls. For instance, CPT soundings carried out across a recent school site identified a loose sand lens at depth that was not visible in trial pits, prompting a switch to a piled solution. Similarly, foundation design for coastal protection works at Balbriggan Harbour relied on in‑situ permeability tests and dynamic probe refusal depths to define the rockhead profile and anchor bond lengths. These examples underline how field testing reduces geological uncertainty and construction risk.

The process begins with a desk study and site walkover, followed by a service‑clearance check in line with Health and Safety Authority guidelines. Testing is conducted from a tracked rig with minimal ground pressure, and real‑time data are screened on‑site to allow immediate adjustment of the investigation depth or test locations. Deliverables include a factual report containing logged profiles, corrected parameters, and graphical plots, together with an interpretative section that feeds directly into laboratory correlation and design recommendations. By integrating in‑situ measurements with selected lab checks—such as Atterberg limits and grain size analysis—we deliver a ground investigation package that meets IS EN 1997 requirements while keeping programme durations and costs under control. For developers and consulting engineers in Balbriggan, this means a single, defensible dataset ready for submission to the building control authority.
